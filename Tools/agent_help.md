First Principles Decomposition
The question “how do I gain access to active deals and who’s funding them” is actually three separate problems that require three separate strategies. Conflating them leads to generic tactics that don’t work at this buyer level.
Problem 1: How do I know which funds are currently deploying into infrastructure in TX/GA/AZ?
Problem 2: How do I know which specific deals are live and in permitting-sensitive territory?
Problem 3: How do I get into the room with the right person at the right moment?
Each has a different access mechanism. Here they are in order of effort vs. payoff.

Layer 1: Public Signal Intelligence — What’s Already Visible
This is the intelligence layer most people ignore because it feels too simple. It isn’t.
FAST-41 Permitting Dashboard — The federal government publishes a live dashboard of major infrastructure projects in the federal permitting pipeline. It lists project name, sponsor, state, and permitting stage. This is literally a list of projects where permitting variance is a current, live concern. It’s free, updated regularly, and almost no one in your buyer universe is systematically monitoring it as a prospecting tool.
State-level environmental and permit dockets — Texas TCEQ, Georgia EPD, and Arizona DEQ all publish permit application queues. A data center or large manufacturing project filing an air permit or water permit in your three states shows up there. That filing means capital has been committed, a developer is identified, and permitting has begun. That’s your entry window.
FERC interconnection queues — Publicly available. Every large load interconnection request in ERCOT (Texas), the Southeast (Georgia), and APS/SRP (Arizona) identifies the project sponsor, the requested load, the county, and the queue position. A project sitting in an interconnection queue with a PE fund as sponsor is a live, capital-committed deal with active permitting exposure.
SEC filings — Infrastructure PE funds with public vehicles (BDCs, REITs, infrastructure funds with SEC-registered components) disclose portfolio acquisitions. Blackstone, Brookfield, KKR, and others file 8-Ks and 10-Qs that describe new platform additions. Not every deal, but enough to surface active deployment patterns.
County assessor and recorder filings — In Texas and Arizona especially, large land acquisitions for industrial use are publicly recorded with buyer entity names. A shell LLC acquiring 200+ acres near a substation in a high-growth county is almost always a PE-backed infrastructure or data center project. This is the earliest possible signal — it precedes permit filing.
The practical implication for you and Alieel: Build a simple weekly monitoring routine across these five sources. It doesn’t require sophisticated tooling. A spreadsheet and two hours per week will surface more actionable deal intelligence than any paid database at this stage.

Layer 2: Paid Intelligence Platforms — What Accelerates the Above
Several platforms aggregate exactly what you need, purpose-built for this. The key is knowing which ones track PE deal flow vs. project development flow — they’re different databases.
Infralogic / Dealogic Infrastructure — Tracks PE infrastructure deal flow, financing rounds, and asset acquisitions. This is the Bloomberg of infrastructure M&A. A principal at Brookfield or Stonepeak appears in here when their fund closes a deal. It’s expensive (~$15-25K/year for institutional access) but if you can get a trial, one session would surface your entire target list of active deals.
DC Velocity / Data Center Hawk — Purpose-built for data center development intelligence. Tracks permit filings, land acquisitions, announced projects, and their sponsors across the US. This is your most direct access to hyperscaler and developer deal flow in your three states.
Reonomy / CoStar — Commercial real estate data that tracks large industrial and commercial land transactions with entity attribution. When a PE-backed developer acquires a site, it appears here with the buyer entity name, which you can then trace to the fund.
Pitchbook — PE deal coverage including infrastructure. Less real-time than Infralogic but more accessible. The key use is tracing portfolio company relationships — if you know Stonepeak owns Platform X, you can track Platform X’s project filings directly.
Wood Mackenzie / BloombergNEF — Energy infrastructure project databases. More useful for manufacturing and grid-scale projects than data centers specifically.
The honest assessment: you don’t need all of these. For your three states and your four project types, DC Hawk for data centers plus FERC queue monitoring plus county recorder tracking covers 70% of what you need at minimal cost.

Layer 3: Human Network Intelligence — The Highest-Value Layer
This is where most people under-invest because it requires relationship building rather than database access. But it’s the only layer that gets you into deals before they’re publicly visible — which is when permitting variance risk is most acute and most valuable to surface.
Infrastructure lawyers are your single best access point to active deal flow. Every PE infrastructure deal in your target markets runs through a small number of law firms — Latham & Watkins, Vinson & Elkins, Kirkland & Ellis, Sidley Austin, and a handful of regional specialists. The infrastructure and energy partners at these firms know which deals are closing, which are in diligence, and which have permitting exposure that’s creating anxiety. They are not the buyer, but they are in the room every day. A relationship with two or three infrastructure partners at these firms is worth more than any database subscription.
The access strategy: these lawyers are consistently on panels at infrastructure conferences. They publish bylined pieces in trade publications. They are approachable through LinkedIn with a credible, non-salesy message about what PFI measures. Your pitch to them is not “subscribe to PFI.” It’s “I’m building the first empirical dataset on permitting variance for infrastructure projects — your clients are likely wrestling with exactly this problem and I’d like to understand what you’re seeing.” That conversation builds relationship and surfaces deal intelligence simultaneously.
Infrastructure lenders — JPMorgan, Bank of America, Citi, and Goldman’s infrastructure lending desks are underwriting these deals right now. The project finance associate or VP at one of these institutions knows every major infrastructure deal in your states that’s currently in financing. They’re also deeply sensitized to permitting risk because their models blow up when timelines move. They are not your buyer — but they can introduce you to the PE fund on the other side of the deal. The access mechanism is the same: conferences, LinkedIn, bylined content.
Development consultants and site selectors — Firms like CBRE, JLL Industrial, Cushman & Wakefield’s data center team, and specialized site selection consultants like DCI are engaged by PE funds and hyperscalers before the permit is filed. They know what’s in the pipeline 12-18 months before it’s public. A relationship with two or three senior people at these firms gives you early-cycle intelligence that no database provides.
Former state economic development officials — Every large manufacturing or data center project in Texas, Georgia, and Arizona goes through the state economic development agency for incentive negotiations before permits are filed. People who recently left TECD (Texas), the Georgia Department of Economic Development, or the Arizona Commerce Authority know the project pipeline intimately and often move into advisory or consulting roles. They are warm introductions to the developers and fund managers behind those projects.

Layer 4: Conference and Publication Presence — Where Deals Are Discussed Publicly
This is the layer that creates ambient presence so that people self-identify to you rather than requiring you to find them.
The three conferences where your specific buyer type — PE infrastructure fund principals and VPs — are concentrated and discussing active deal challenges:
Infrastructure Investor Global Summit (typically March, New York) — The premier gathering of PE infrastructure fund managers globally. This is where Brookfield, KKR, Stonepeak, Apollo infrastructure teams are all in one room for two days.
IMN Infrastructure Forum — More transactional, more deal-flow oriented. Attendees are actively deploying capital.
Data Center World / DatacenterDynamics Invest — Where hyperscaler infrastructure teams and digital infrastructure PE investors converge specifically on the project type with the highest permitting friction.
The strategy at these events is not to pitch. It’s to run a breakfast or side session on permitting variance specifically — “What does the data actually show about permitting timelines in high-growth states?” — and invite 8-10 relevant people. That format positions you as the authority, surfaces the most engaged buyers in a single session, and generates the warm relationships that lead to presell conversations over the following weeks.
